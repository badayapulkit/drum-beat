# Framework — Launch Tiering

A derivative description, in our own words, of the Launch Tiering
methodology widely taught in PMM programs (Product Marketing Alliance
and similar). Inspired by, not copied from, the original.

## The idea

Not all launches deserve equal investment. Treat every launch as Tier 1
and you exhaust the company; treat every launch as Tier 3 and you
under-invest in the launches that matter most. Tiering aligns
investment to expected impact.

## Three tiers

### Tier 1 — company-defining launch
- New product, new market, or category-defining feature
- Material to annual revenue or strategic position
- All-hands: PMM, sales, CS, support, partner, exec sponsorship
- Multi-quarter prep window
- Full asset coverage + amplification campaign + exec involvement

### Tier 2 — meaningful launch
- New feature in existing product or expansion to adjacent market
- Meaningful to a quarterly KPI or audience segment
- Cross-functional but PMM-led
- Weeks of prep
- Standard asset pack + segmented amplification

### Tier 3 — incremental launch
- Iteration on an existing product or quality-of-life improvement
- Marginal revenue impact or narrow audience
- PMM-only or PMM + one team
- Days of prep
- Lightweight assets (release note, single social post, in-app update)

## How to tier in practice

Weigh seven dimensions:

1. Revenue impact
2. Audience reach
3. Strategic priority (named on top-level OKRs?)
4. Novelty
5. Cross-functional dependencies
6. Competitive stakes
7. Realistic prep window

If 4+ dimensions point to Tier 1: Tier 1. If 4+ point to Tier 3: Tier 3.
Otherwise: Tier 2 (the most common outcome).

## Common mistakes

- **Tier inflation** — every launch is Tier 1. Burns exec attention,
  trains the org to ignore the next "big" launch.
- **Tier deflation** — under-investing in a launch with real
  competitive or strategic stakes.
- **Tiering by feature size, not impact** — a small UX change can be
  Tier 2 if it unblocks a major segment; a big engineering project can
  be Tier 3 if customers won't notice.

## Why this framework matters for Drumbeat

Tier classification is the *first* AI step in the Drumbeat workflow
because it sets:

- Asset depth (how comprehensive each generated asset should be)
- Asset coverage (which of the 6 standard assets to skip)
- Tone (Tier 1 = strategic and ambitious; Tier 3 = factual and short)
- Resourcing recommendations to surface to the user
