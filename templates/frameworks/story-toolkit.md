# Framework — Story Toolkit

Derivative description of narrative structure for launch comms. Drawn
from PMM training and classical narrative structure (problem → stakes →
solution → proof). Our own words.

## The idea

Launch comms that read like feature lists fail. Launch comms that read
like *stories* land. Story structure makes content memorable, quotable,
and shareable.

The Story Toolkit gives every long-form asset (blog, internal
announcement, founder LinkedIn post) the same four-beat narrative
spine.

## The four beats

### 1. Problem
What painful situation does the customer live in today? Specific. In
their language, not yours.

> Example: "Solo PMMs spend three weeks on launch prep — positioning,
> messaging, sales enablement, blog, social — for every launch, every
> quarter."

### 2. Stakes
What does the status quo cost? Why does this matter now?

> Example: "While the PMM is buried in launch prep, three other launches
> stack up behind it. The team ships fewer launches, each one rougher
> than it should be. The CEO notices."

### 3. Solution
What changed? What did you build? Show, don't tell — use a specific
scenario, not a feature list.

> Example: "Drumbeat takes a one-page brief and produces a
> tier-classified launch package — six assets, on-message — in under
> 30 minutes. The PMM reviews and ships, instead of writing from
> scratch."

### 4. Proof
Why should anyone believe you? Customer quote, metric, before/after,
or honest beta framing.

> Example: "Ten design partners cut average launch prep from six weeks
> to nine days. Quote from one: '{quote}.'"

## When to use the toolkit

Every long-form asset should follow this spine:

- Blog post: each section maps to a beat
- Internal announcement: compressed version (1 sentence per beat)
- Founder LinkedIn post: hook with the problem, land on the proof
- Customer email: shortened — opens on the problem, lands on the
  solution + CTA

Short-form assets (single tweet, sales talking points) don't need the
full spine — but they should still ladder back to one of the beats
(usually problem or proof).

## Common mistakes

- **Skipping the stakes.** If you don't earn the reader's "why does
  this matter?" you'll lose them at the solution.
- **Solution without problem.** Feature lists pretending to be stories.
- **Vague proof.** "Customers love it." End the story honestly: "We're
  shipping with N design partners; results in M weeks."
- **Solution that sounds like every other product.** If you can swap
  your product name for a competitor's and the paragraph still works,
  you're not telling a story — you're listing features.

## How Drumbeat uses the Story Toolkit

The Story Toolkit governs the structure of long-form generated assets.
The blog post prompt, internal announcement prompt, and founder
LinkedIn variant all reference this framework. The system instructions
explicitly require the four beats in every long-form asset.
