# Framework — Positioning

Derivative description of the positioning methodology widely taught in
PMM programs. Inspired by April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome," PMA
positioning training, and similar sources. Our own words.

## The idea

Positioning is the deliberate act of defining where your product sits
in the customer's mind. Not "what your product does" — "what category
it competes in, who it's for, and what unique value it delivers vs.
the alternatives."

Strong positioning makes downstream marketing easier (messaging,
content, sales). Weak positioning makes everything harder.

## The five components

1. **Competitive alternatives** — what customers would use if you
   didn't exist. (Not just direct competitors — also spreadsheets,
   manual processes, doing nothing.)
2. **Unique attributes** — what your product has that the alternatives
   don't.
3. **Value (benefits)** — what those unique attributes enable for the
   customer that they actually care about.
4. **Best-fit customers** — who, specifically, gets the most value from
   those benefits.
5. **Market category** — the frame of reference you want customers to
   use when thinking about your product. (You can pick the category;
   you don't have to accept the default.)

## Positioning statement template

> For {best-fit customer} who {has problem / job-to-be-done},
> {Product} is a {market category} that {key benefit}.
> Unlike {competitive alternatives}, {Product} {unique
> differentiator}.

## Common mistakes

- **Positioning by feature.** "We have AI" is not positioning. AI is a
  feature; positioning answers what category you compete in and what
  unique value the customer gets.
- **Picking the wrong category.** If you're priced like a $50/mo SaaS
  but positioned in the "enterprise platform" category, you'll lose
  every comparison. Pick the category where your unique attributes
  matter most.
- **"Best-fit customer = everyone."** Then you serve no one well.
- **Over-rotating on competitors.** Positioning is about what *you*
  uniquely deliver, not a list of competitor weaknesses.

## How Drumbeat uses positioning

When the brief lacks clear positioning, Drumbeat surfaces the gap
*before* generating the messaging matrix — because messaging derived
from weak positioning is fragile. The brief intake explicitly asks for
differentiation and competitive alternatives, and the messaging matrix
prompt verifies positioning is concrete enough to anchor each row.
